This 10-minute introduction sets the stage using a gamified escape room premise. Participants enter The Realm of the Learning Vault — a fantasy world where TD professionals must unlock three chambers using the power of AI-assisted design. The PACE framework is their master key.

PACE Framework — Master Key
Chamber I · PACE: Purpose
🔮 The Enchanter's Workshop — AI Governance & Visual Assets
⏱ 0:10 – 0:35 · 25 min P — Purpose 🎤 Facilitator A
Learning Objectives
1
Identify at least two AI governance principles that apply to learning asset creation.
2
Write a purposeful, specific prompt in CoPilot and generate a visual asset concept.
3
Connect prompt quality to output quality: "what you put in is what you get out."
4
Classify content before selecting an AI tool using the asset first, tool second decision framework.
Determine what the asset needs to do before choosing which AI tool to use — matching the tool to the task, not the other way around.
5
Recognize that the content of a prompt is a governance decision — what goes in, and whether it belongs there.
Every prompt is a choice about what information to share with an AI system. That choice is a governance act with real consequences for privacy, accuracy, and organizational trust.
6
Connect input quality to output quality using the garbage in, garbage out principle as a governance standard.
Low-quality, vague, or inappropriate inputs don't just produce weak outputs — they represent a governance failure. Prompt quality is a professional standard, not just a productivity tip.

Talking Points — AI Governance

1
What is AI governance in L&D?
Organizational and ethical guardrails: data privacy, copyright, accuracy, transparency, bias awareness. We use AI as a collaborator, not a replacement decision-maker.
2
Responsible AI use — 3-point checklist
(a) Never input confidential or PII data into public AI tools. (b) Disclose AI-assisted content per your org policy. (c) You are responsible for the final output — AI is a tool, you are the author.
3
Introducing CoPilot — your AI collaborator today
Brief orientation: copilot.microsoft.com. What it does well for L&D — content drafting, script writing, design briefs. Today we pair it with CoPilot to build visual and written assets.
4
What you put in is what you get out
Vague prompt = vague output. Show contrast: "Write me a training activity" vs. a specific RTCF-structured prompt. Note: Module 2 will teach RTCF in depth — plant the seed here.

🔑 RTCF Framework — Full Facilitator Walkthrough
6 min
🎤 How to Deliver This Section

Walk through each letter one at a time. Spend ~90 seconds per letter: give the definition, say the scripted line, show the example. Goal: by the end of this walkthrough, participants can construct their own RTCF prompt independently. Do not rush — this is the intellectual core of Module 2.

Suggested pacing: R (90s) → T (90s) → C (90s) → F (90s) → Full example (60s) = 6 min total.

💬 Opening Script

"Module 1 introduced the idea that what you put in is what you get out. Now I'm giving you the formula that makes that real. It's called RTCF — Role, Task, Context, Format. These four elements are what separate a prompt that produces a usable first draft from one you spend 30 minutes fixing."

R
Role — Who should CoPilot be?
Tell CoPilot what expertise and authority to bring to the task. A role activates a specific tone, vocabulary, and decision-making frame — like briefing a specialist before they start work.
🎤 Say This

"'You are an instructional designer' gives CoPilot a completely different lens than 'You are a marketing copywriter' — even for the same topic. Role is the briefing that frames everything that follows."

Examples: instructional designer · L&D consultant · podcast producer · video scriptwriter · facilitator coach
T
Task — What should CoPilot produce?
Specify the exact asset type, length, and purpose. The more precise your task, the less editing you'll need. Name the deliverable explicitly — do not make CoPilot guess what you want.
🎤 Say This

"Compare 'write a training script' to 'write a 90-second video script with an opening hook, three key learning points, and a close directing learners to a resource.' Same request — completely different output."

Examples: write a facilitator guide intro · draft a podcast script · create a design brief · outline a job aid
C
Context — Who is this for and why?
Include audience, experience level, tone, modality, constraints, and any org culture cues. Context is where most prompts fall short — the more specific you are, the more targeted the output becomes.
🎤 Say This

"'For new employees' is context. 'For new employees in their first 30 days who have never worked in a corporate environment and may have anxiety around performance expectations' — that's context that changes the output completely."

Include: audience · experience level · modality · tone · org culture · sensitive topics to avoid
F
Format — How should it be structured?
Tell CoPilot how to present and organize the output. Format instructions reduce editing time and make the CoPilot handoff much smoother. If you don't specify, CoPilot will choose — and it may not match your workflow.
🎤 Say This

"'Format this as a two-column script: Column 1 = on-screen visuals, Column 2 = narrator voiceover' is a format instruction. 'Format as a structured design brief' is another. Without it, you get a wall of text."

Examples: bulleted outline · two-column script · structured design brief · numbered steps · slide-by-slide breakdown
🎤 Walk-Through — Full Assembled RTCF Prompt (read aloud, identify each component)
[R] You are an instructional designer specializing in corporate learning and development. [T] Write a 90-second video script for a microlearning module on giving effective feedback. [C] Audience: mid-level managers, ages 28-45, retail company. Comfortable with tech but little formal training experience. Tone: warm, direct, practical. Avoid corporate jargon. [F] Two-column script: Column 1 = on-screen visuals/action, Column 2 = narrator voiceover. Hook in first 10 seconds. Call-to-action in final 10 seconds.
💬 Say After

"That's one prompt. Four components. That's the difference between a draft you can actually use and one you spend 30 minutes fixing. Now let's look at the RTCF framework card — this is what we're posting in Teams for you to keep."

Content Delivery — AI Integration
2 min

Talking Points — Microsoft CoPilot Integration

1
How Microsoft CoPilot and Microsoft 365 work together
Microsoft CoPilot is embedded directly into the Microsoft 365 ecosystem — Word, PowerPoint, Teams, Outlook, and more. This means AI-assisted content generation happens inside the tools your organization already uses. You prompt CoPilot to draft, summarize, reformat, or build — and the output lives right where you need it, no copy-paste workflow required. For L&D professionals, this means facilitator guides in Word, scripts in PowerPoint, communication in Teams — all augmented by AI from within your existing workflow.
2
Why a framework matters
Without PACE + RTCF, AI-assisted design is random. These frameworks give you consistency. ADDIE/SAM logic still applies — AI compresses the timeline within it, it doesn't replace the model.
3
Iteration is design, not failure
Your first prompt output is a first draft. Expert AI users iterate 2–5 times. "Add a more conversational tone." "Shorten by 30%." "Make the opening line a question." This is workflow, not troubleshooting.
